12 Assets, No Visibility - What Is Actually Underperforming?

A European renewable developer with 730 MW across three countries could not answer a basic question from its board: which assets are underperforming, and why. This case structures the data, defines the framework, and surfaces where the money is being lost.

The analysis identifies a €4.49M annual revenue gap, with one asset alone contributing more than 80% of the shortfall.

Context

Why this problem matters

European renewable developers are scaling faster than their management infrastructure. A developer that operated two or three assets in 2015 may now manage twelve or twenty across multiple countries - each with its own country team, its own reporting format, and its own definition of what good performance looks like.

This fragmentation is the default state of most mid-sized renewable portfolios today. The consequence is that underperformance goes undetected for months, root causes get misdiagnosed, and management decisions - on O&M contracts, TSO engagement, capital allocation - get made without a shared analytical foundation.

This case examines exactly that situation: a 730 MW portfolio across Germany, the Netherlands, and Denmark where the data existed but no framework existed to make it speak. The analysis moves from a raw set of disconnected numbers to a prioritised set of actions - and shows how the same symptom in two different assets turns out to have two completely different causes.

Data Foundation

Data sources and their role in this analysis

Asset-level operational data for private renewable portfolios is proprietary and not publicly available. The approach taken here uses verified public benchmarks as the reference framework and constructs asset-level figures consistent with those benchmarks and with published operator disclosures - standard practice in portfolio benchmarking work.

The Situation

What the company was facing

The developer's asset management team tracked performance across twelve assets using disconnected Excel files, each maintained independently by country teams in Germany, the Netherlands, and Denmark. There was no common KPI definition. Germany measured availability differently from Denmark. The Netherlands team tracked curtailment hours; the German team did not.

"The board asked which assets were underperforming. The team had twelve different spreadsheets and no common definition of underperformance."

When the board asked, ahead of a mid-year investor review, which assets were underperforming and what was being done about it - the asset management team had no answer that held across the portfolio. Individual asset managers knew their own numbers. Nobody had the full picture.

The immediate question was: across 730 MW of wind and solar in three markets, where is performance falling short of what these assets should be delivering, and what is actually causing it? The portfolio below is the starting point. Understanding its composition is necessary before any comparison is meaningful because a 2015 German onshore asset and a 2023 Danish offshore installation cannot be judged against the same number.

The core business problem is not the absence of data. It is the absence of a consistent performance framework across assets, leading to delayed identification of underperformance and misdirected operational focus.

Analysis · Step 1

Understanding the portfolio before comparing it

The first thing the analysis established was the portfolio's own internal structure - before any external benchmark was applied. Three observations from the composition alone shaped the rest of the work.

Denmark holds 270 MW in just two assets, meaning a single event at Anholt East has an outsized portfolio effect. Germany has six assets but only 245 MW combined - less than Nordsee Alpha and Anholt East together. The portfolio's financial weight sits heavily in offshore wind, which carries capacity factors of 38-40% versus 23-29% for onshore - but also higher exposure to grid curtailment, which in 2023 became a material issue in the Dutch market specifically.

Analysis · Step 2

Which assets are not generating what they should?

The performance metric used is capacity factor, which measures actual generation as a share of theoretical maximum. The reference point is not a portfolio average but the country and technology specific benchmark drawn from Bruegel and ENTSO-E data. This distinction matters: German onshore wind has a lower benchmark CF than Danish offshore not because it is performing poorly, but because it operates in lower-wind inland locations. A single portfolio average would systematically penalise assets in stronger wind markets while masking genuine underperformance in weaker ones.

Capacity factor benchmarking is constructed using country and technology specific reference values. Each asset is evaluated against its relevant peer group rather than a single portfolio average, ensuring that differences in wind resource, technology maturity, asset age, and geography are correctly accounted for.

Three assets show gaps of more than three percentage points below their respective benchmarks. Two are German onshore assets commissioned in 2015 and 2016. The third - and the one that prompted the most scrutiny - is Nordsee Alpha, a Dutch offshore installation commissioned in 2023. A brand-new asset underperforming its benchmark by 3.8 percentage points is not what the development team expected.

Three assets below threshold Elbewind (Germany, commissioned 2015): 18.4% actual vs 23.0% benchmark - a gap of 4.6pp on an asset now in its ninth year of operation. Aging equipment is the likely factor, but the scale of the gap warrants a formal O&M investigation.

Analysis · Step 3

The same gap, two completely different problems

A capacity factor shortfall is a symptom. It does not identify the cause. The next step was to disaggregate downtime into four categories: planned maintenance (scheduled, managed within O&M contracts), unplanned mechanical (equipment failures and unscheduled stops), grid curtailment (TSO instruction to limit output), and weather or force majeure. The reason for this separation is straightforward - the O&M contractor is responsible for mechanical reliability but has no role in grid curtailment, which is a TSO and regulatory matter.

The WindEurope O&M benchmark for onshore wind sets unplanned mechanical downtime at approximately 263 hours per year. Assets significantly above this figure have an internal operations problem. Assets whose downtime profile is dominated by curtailment have an infrastructure and regulatory problem.

The timing of downtime events also matters. Mechanical failures tend to be sporadic and asset-specific, while curtailment follows system-wide grid congestion patterns. This distinction determines whether the response lies within the operator's control or requires regulatory engagement.

What the breakdown reveals Elbewind's 480 hours of unplanned mechanical downtime - nearly double the WindEurope benchmark of 263 hours - confirms that its underperformance is an O&M reliability problem. The contractor needs to be challenged on component failure rates and response SLAs. The 2015 vintage suggests drivetrain and gearbox wear may be driving the elevated downtime.

Nordsee Alpha's picture is entirely different. Its unplanned mechanical hours are elevated but not exceptional. The dominant source of its lost generation is 420 hours of grid curtailment - the TSO instructing the asset to reduce output because the onshore transmission network cannot absorb what the asset is generating. This is a documented structural issue across Dutch offshore wind in 2023, confirmed in RWE's annual disclosure. The correct response is regulatory engagement and curtailment compensation negotiation with the TSO - not a conversation with the O&M team. Treating these two assets as the same type of problem, based only on their CF gaps, would have directed effort and budget at the wrong place.
Analysis · Step 4

What it costs in euros

Capacity factor gaps and downtime hours are the right language for operational discussions. For the board and for investors, the relevant unit is revenue. Translating the operational picture into financial terms also changes the priority order in a way that percentage gaps alone do not show.

Revenue is modelled as generation multiplied by a technology-specific capture price. Renewable assets do not receive the full average wholesale price - solar generation concentrates at midday and depresses prices at precisely the hours it produces most; offshore wind spreads generation more evenly but still faces partial cannibalisation. Capture rates applied are Solar PV at 85%, Offshore Wind at 90%, and Onshore Wind at 92% of the average wholesale price, following the Bruegel EU Renewables Value Tracker methodology. Wholesale price references are from ENTSO-E and Ember: Germany €95/MWh, Netherlands €100/MWh, Denmark €90/MWh.

Why scale changes the priority order Elbewind has a larger percentage CF gap than Nordsee Alpha - 4.6pp versus 3.8pp. In a purely operational ranking, Elbewind looks worse. But Nordsee Alpha is a 120 MW offshore asset at €100/MWh. Elbewind is a 30 MW onshore asset at €95/MWh. Both are real - but they are not equally urgent, and the financial translation makes that visible in a way that percentage gaps alone do not.
Recommendations

What the analysis points to

Four actions follow directly from the findings. They are ordered by financial impact and controllability, not operational complexity. Large-scale revenue losses with clear external drivers are addressed first, followed by operational improvements within the company's control.

1
Immediate

Escalate Nordsee Alpha curtailment with the TSO

420 hours of curtailment in the first year of a new offshore installation represents €3.62M in lost revenue. The TSO should be engaged on curtailment compensation under current Dutch regulatory provisions. If curtailment continues at this level into Year 2, the issue should be escalated through industry channels. This is not an O&M problem and should not be treated as one.

Expected impact: recovery of €2M to €3M annually through curtailment compensation or reduced grid restrictions, depending on regulatory outcome.

2
Short Term

Conduct formal O&M review for Elbewind and Nordwind I

Two 2015 to 2016 vintage assets both showing unplanned mechanical hours well above the WindEurope benchmark is a pattern that warrants formal review. Both assets should undergo technical audit. The case for targeted repowering, including replacement of aging drivetrain and rotor components, should be assessed against the cost of continued degraded performance at the current O&M contract terms.

Expected impact: €0.8M to €1.2M annual recovery through reduced unplanned downtime and improved availability.

3
Medium Term

Standardise KPI definitions across all country teams

The reason the board had no answer is not the absence of data. It is the absence of common definitions. Six KPIs should be standardised across Germany, the Netherlands, and Denmark: capacity factor vs benchmark, mechanical availability, unplanned downtime hours, grid curtailment hours, generation vs budget, and capture price vs market average. Monthly unified reporting against these definitions becomes the minimum operating standard.

Expected impact: faster identification of performance drift and fewer delayed escalations, reducing the time from issue detection to management action.

4
Ongoing

Set early warning thresholds to shift from reactive to proactive monitoring

Problems detected at board review rather than when they first appear are a structural monitoring failure. Alert thresholds should be set at asset level: CF more than 3pp below monthly benchmark triggers automatic review; unplanned mechanical hours above 50 per month flags for O&M escalation. This analysis is the diagnostic foundation; the monitoring system it should lead to prevents the same conversation from being needed again in twelve months.

Expected impact: earlier intervention on asset underperformance, lower revenue leakage, and more consistent board-level reporting.

Model Notes

Assumptions and limitations

The analysis is based on a constructed asset-level dataset calibrated to public benchmarks. Actual asset performance data is proprietary and not publicly disclosed.

Capacity factor benchmarks are derived from country-level and technology-level averages. They may not fully capture micro-location differences such as wind resource variability, site-specific constraints, turbine model differences, or local grid conditions.

Revenue calculations assume stable wholesale prices and fixed capture rates. In reality, price volatility, cannibalisation effects, and power purchase agreement terms may alter realised revenues.

Curtailment estimates are based on publicly reported market conditions and comparable operator disclosures. Actual curtailment exposure may vary depending on grid conditions, dispatch rules, and compensation arrangements.
