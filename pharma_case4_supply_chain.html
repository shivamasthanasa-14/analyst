Fixing the Supply Chain Before the Product Runs Out - Stockout Analysis Across Four Markets

A generic pharmaceutical company had experienced seven stockout events across Germany, France, the Netherlands, and Poland in eighteen months. Demand forecasting was done at the European level. Each market managed its own inventory independently. Nobody saw the shortage coming until it had already arrived.

The improved planning model reduces projected stockouts from 4.7 to 0.8 per year and protects roughly €3.4M of annual revenue for an added working-capital cost of €0.9M.

Context

Why this problem matters

Generic pharmaceutical supply chains operate on margins that leave almost no tolerance for inefficiency. A branded product company can sometimes absorb a stockout by redirecting commercial effort; for a generic manufacturer, a stockout means a competitor's product fills the gap immediately and irreversibly - pharmacies substitution-switch, and returning to pre-stockout volume can take months or never happen at all.

Seven stockout events in eighteen months for a single product is not a run of bad luck. At the frequency recorded here, it is a structural failure of the supply planning process. The EMA's shortage reporting database and academic analysis of European pharmaceutical supply chain failures consistently identify three root causes: demand forecasting that does not account for market-specific drivers, inventory policies set at an aggregate level that mask country-level exposure, and lead time assumptions that do not reflect actual supplier performance.

The product in this analysis is a chronic disease generic, a cardiovascular agent used for long-term patient management, with a relatively stable but not static demand profile. It is not an emergency medicine, but its absence creates substitution events that are difficult and costly to reverse. The company had invested in ERP-based supply planning, but had not built the analytical layer that would flag country-level demand divergence before stock ran out.

The Situation

What the company was facing

Between January 2022 and June 2023, the company experienced seven stockout events: three in Germany, two in France, one in the Netherlands, and one in Poland. Each event lasted between 12 and 47 days. Each was identified reactively - by a country sales manager receiving a complaint from a distribution customer or a wholesale account flagging an inability to fulfil pharmacy orders.

"The European supply plan showed inventory above the minimum target every month except the month the stockout happened. The problem was always a surprise, and it was never supposed to be."

The supply planning process operated as follows: the central supply chain team produced a 12-month rolling demand forecast at the European level, which was then split by country using a fixed market-share allocation based on the previous year's sales. Each country received a quarterly shipment allocation based on this split. Country inventory levels were monitored by local teams against a minimum stock threshold set at four weeks of average demand. The problem with this system is visible in retrospect: it used last year's market shares to allocate this year's inventory, it set the safety stock threshold based on average demand without accounting for demand variability, and it set lead times in the model at the contractual norm rather than the actual supplier performance.

Analysis · Step 1

Where is the demand forecast going wrong?

The first question was whether the stockouts were caused by demand being systematically higher than forecast, by demand being highly variable but accurately forecast on average, or by both. A MAPE of 31% at the European level obscures what is happening at country level - and it is at country level that inventory is either present or absent.

The demand time series was decomposed by country using 18 months of sales-out data from IQVIA MIDAS. The decomposition separated trend, seasonality, and irregular components. The European-level forecast used a simple linear trend with no seasonality adjustment. Germany showed a pronounced Q1 uplift - consistent with chronic disease restocking dynamics at the start of the insurance year - that was absent from France and the Netherlands but present in an attenuated form in Poland.

Germany's Q1 uplift is the primary forecast failure Germany shows a consistent 35–45% demand increase in Q1 (January–March) relative to the annual average - driven by the annual reset of statutory insurance co-payment thresholds and patient restocking at the start of the benefit year. This pattern is documented in German pharmacy dispensing research and is predictable from the calendar. The European-level forecast model captured none of it. Every one of Germany's three stockout events occurred in Q1 or early Q2 - after the demand spike had depleted safety stock and before the quarterly replenishment shipment arrived.
Analysis · Step 2

Is the safety stock level actually adequate?

A four-week safety stock threshold set against average demand is a design choice that works only if demand is relatively stable and supplier lead times are predictable. If demand is variable and lead times are variable, a safety stock calculated from averages will systematically under-protect against the tail risk that actually causes stockouts.

The correct safety stock for a given market is a function of demand variability, lead time variability, and target service level. The model uses a 95% service level and calculates safety stock using weekly demand standard deviation, average replenishment lead time, lead time standard deviation, and the z-score for the target service level. This makes the policy responsive to market risk rather than applying the same four-week rule everywhere.

The current four-week flat policy is adequate only for the Netherlands and arguably for France; it is materially under-protective for Germany and Poland.
Analysis · Step 3

What is supplier lead time actually doing?

The supply plan used a contractual lead time of 8 weeks from API supplier to finished goods delivery. Actual lead time performance over the 24-month analysis period told a different story: average actual lead time was 11.2 weeks, with a standard deviation of 2.8 weeks and individual observations ranging from 7 to 18 weeks. A supply plan calibrated to an 8-week lead time, receiving inventory on an 11-week average schedule, is structurally short of stock 100% of the time.

The plan was using a lead time that was almost never achieved The 8-week contractual lead time was achieved in only 12% of observed replenishment cycles. The distribution is right-skewed, with a meaningful tail extending to 16–18 weeks - driven by API batch testing delays at the supplier and customs clearance delays at the Polish border. A supply plan that uses the 8-week contractual lead time as its planning assumption is operating in a state of permanent optimism. The correct planning lead time - the P80 of the observed distribution - is 14 weeks. Planning to 14 weeks would require a larger average inventory position but would eliminate the lead time mismatch as a structural cause of stockouts.
Analysis · Step 4

What does the improved model predict - and what does it cost?

The three root causes identified - inadequate demand forecasting (MAPE 31% vs target 10–15%), safety stock policy calibrated to average demand rather than variability, and lead time assumptions calibrated to contract rather than actual performance - were each modelled independently and then in combination to estimate the service level improvement and the inventory cost of remediation.

The combined scenario models what happens if the demand forecast is rebuilt at country level with seasonality, safety stock is recalculated using the statistical model at 95% service level, and the planning lead time is updated to the P80 of the observed distribution. The cost of the higher average inventory position - additional working capital tied up in safety stock - is compared against the revenue loss avoided.

Recommendations

What the analysis points to

Actions are prioritised by urgency and ease of implementation. Calendar-driven German stockouts can be addressed immediately, while structural forecasting and safety stock redesign follow as system improvements.

1
Immediate

Pre-build Q1 German inventory before October - implement as a standing annual instruction

Germany's Q1 demand spike is predictable, calendar-driven, and responsible for all three German stockout events. The supply chain team should place a supplementary Q4 order each year, sized to cover the expected Q1 uplift above average demand based on the country-level seasonal model. The deadlines: supplementary order placed no later than October 1; stock in German warehouse by December 15. This is the single highest-impact action and can be implemented before any system change. It requires no new budget approval because it accelerates inventory build by one quarter.

Expected impact: prevent the recurring German Q1 stockout pattern and protect roughly €1.5M of annual revenue.

2
Immediate

Update planning lead time from 8 weeks (contract) to 14 weeks (P80 actual)

The 8-week contractual lead time is achieved in 12% of cycles. Using it as the planning assumption guarantees a structural inventory shortfall. The ERP planning parameters should be updated immediately to use a 14-week planning lead time for all four markets. This will trigger an initial larger replenishment order as the system recalibrates to the new assumption - which is intentional. The finance team should be briefed on the one-time working capital increase (~€0.4M over two quarters) before the parameter change is made.

Expected impact: remove the structural planning mismatch that caused the ERP to assume replenishment earlier than it actually arrived.

3
Short Term

Rebuild country-level demand forecasts with seasonality and replace the European top-down allocation

The current European-level forecast split by fixed market share is the methodological root cause of the forecast error. Country-level forecasts should be built independently for each market, using at minimum a simple seasonal decomposition (Holt-Winters or equivalent) calibrated to the market-specific drivers documented in this analysis. The technical implementation can be done in the company's existing ERP or in a spreadsheet-based planning tool while the ERP model is updated. The key behavioral change is that country-level demand input should come from the country commercial teams, not be derived mechanically from the European total.

Expected impact: reduce forecast MAPE from 31% toward the 10-15% planning target for stable chronic-disease generics.

4
Medium Term

Implement statistical safety stock policy - differentiated by market based on variability and lead time

The flat four-week safety stock policy should be replaced by a market-specific policy calculated from the statistical model documented in this analysis. Germany: 8.4 weeks. Poland: 7.1 weeks. France: 5.2 weeks. Netherlands: 4.8 weeks. These levels should be reviewed annually using the rolling 24-month demand and lead time data. Building the safety stock recalculation as a quarterly management information output, alongside the MAPE tracking, ensures the policy adapts to changing market conditions rather than remaining fixed at a level set in a different demand environment.

Expected impact: reduce annual projected stockouts to 0.8 per year while keeping incremental working capital within the €0.9M modelled cost.

Model Notes

Assumptions and limitations

Demand and stockout data are illustrative and calibrated to public shortage and generic-market benchmarks. Actual implementation would require ERP, sales-out, wholesaler, and supplier delivery data at SKU and country level.

Revenue protected is estimated from avoided stockout volume multiplied by net selling price. It excludes longer-term substitution losses, payer penalties, and patient switching behaviour after shortages.

The safety stock model assumes normally distributed demand and lead time variability. In practice, extreme events such as supplier quality holds or regulatory disruptions would require separate scenario buffers.

The combined model assumes no improvement in supplier lead time. Supplier performance improvement would lower working-capital requirements, but should be treated as a separate negotiation and quality workstream.
