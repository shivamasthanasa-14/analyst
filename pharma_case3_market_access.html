Can We Afford to Launch? - HTA Mapping and Pricing Scenarios for a Specialty Drug

A specialty drug preparing to launch in Germany, France, and the Netherlands faced three different HTA frameworks and price referencing dynamics that would lock the global price anchor for years. The scenarios had not been built.

The sequencing decision changes the 5-year EU revenue case by roughly €630M under base-case HTA assumptions, making launch order a strategic pricing decision rather than an operational timeline choice.

Context

Why this problem matters

Launching a specialty pharmaceutical product in Europe is not a single decision - it is a sequence of interlocking country decisions, each of which creates constraints on what is possible in the next market. Germany has no formal price control at launch but requires a health technology assessment (HTA) within twelve months. France conducts a full HTA before reimbursement is granted, with price negotiated afterward. The Netherlands uses a conditional reimbursement framework with mandatory outcome-based agreements for high-cost drugs. Each system values evidence differently, each negotiates price through different channels, and each feeds into an international reference pricing (IRP) network that links prices across 26 European markets.

For a company launching a specialty drug - one with a high list price justified by a small patient population and a significant clinical benefit claim - the market access sequencing decision is as commercially consequential as the clinical dossier itself. Launch too early in France, accept too low a price to achieve reimbursement, and that price becomes the reference floor for Austria, Belgium, and twelve other markets. Launch too late in Germany, and a full year of revenue at the uncapped German price is foregone.

This case examines the situation facing a specialty oncology product's market access team eighteen months before a planned launch. The clinical dossier was complete, but the commercial risk was unresolved: country HTA strategies had not been mapped against each other, and the pricing scenarios had not been stress-tested against the IRP cascade.

The Situation

What the market access team was facing

The product - a specialty oncology agent targeting a validated biomarker present in approximately 8% of a specific solid tumour population - had received a positive CHMP opinion from the EMA. A European launch was planned within 18 months. Germany, France, and the Netherlands were identified as the priority launch markets, accounting for an estimated 58% of addressable European patient volume and anchoring the IRP reference basket for the remaining 23 European markets.

"The clinical team had modelled the trial. The commercial team had modelled peak sales. Nobody had modelled what happens to the price if France grants ASMR III and Austria references it before Germany negotiates."

Three planning gaps were identified at the outset. First, the HTA benefit assessments for each country had been developed in functional silos - the German medical team had built the AMNOG dossier strategy, the French regulatory team had mapped the HAS submission, and the Netherlands access team was designing the managed entry agreement - but no one had mapped the three country strategies against each other for consistency and sequencing risk.

Second, the revenue model used a single price assumption across all European markets, treating the IRP cascade as a risk footnote rather than a modelling input. Third, the launch sequence - Germany first, then France and Netherlands simultaneously - had been chosen for operational reasons without explicit analysis of what each sequencing option would do to the price corridor in reference markets.

Analysis · Step 1

Mapping the HTA landscape - what each country will actually assess

The three HTA frameworks differ not just in process but in what they consider evidence of value. Germany's G-BA assesses whether there is added benefit over the appropriate comparator - a binary-ish question that determines the negotiating mandate the GKV-Spitzenverband takes into AMNOG discussions. France's HAS assesses both medical benefit (SMR) and therapeutic added value (ASMR, rated I–V), where the ASMR rating directly determines the price corridor CEPS will accept. The Netherlands' Zorginstituut assesses cost-effectiveness and conditions reimbursement on outcome agreements.

For this product, the core clinical evidence - a randomised Phase III trial showing a 38% improvement in progression-free survival over second-line standard of care, with overall survival data not yet mature - lands differently in each framework. In Germany, immature OS data against the G-BA comparator creates benefit rating risk. In France, PFS as the primary endpoint creates an ASMR III or IV outcome risk rather than ASMR I or II. In the Netherlands, the absence of a mature cost-effectiveness model creates a requirement for a managed entry agreement.

The ASMR IV (minor improvement) risk is non-trivial given immature OS data. Each rating scenario feeds a different net price, and those net prices feed the IRP cascade.
Analysis · Step 2

What does competitive benchmarking show about achievable price?

Before building the IRP cascade, the range of achievable net prices in each market was estimated using analogue analysis from eight comparable oncology launches between 2019 and 2023. Analogue selection used four criteria: therapy-area adjacency, PFS primary endpoint, immature OS at launch, and similar eligible patient population. Each analogue was mapped to HTA outcome and net price to create the scenario inputs.

Analysis · Step 3

IRP cascade - what does each scenario do to the European price corridor?

International reference pricing means that the price accepted in one market propagates to others through a defined reference basket. The cascade model built here traces three scenarios - optimistic HTA outcomes, base case, and conservative - through the 26-market IRP network, estimating the effective net revenue per patient in each market given the reference price each would apply.

The model uses the EURIPID reference basket weighting for each European market. Where confidential net prices are relevant (Germany post-AMNOG, France post-CEPS), a disclosure assumption is applied: Germany's net AMNOG prices are confidential but frequently estimated within a ±10% range; France's CEPS net prices are partially disclosed under transparency regulations.

Recommendations

What the analysis points to

Recommendations are prioritised by price-anchor risk, not by launch convenience. The objective is to protect the European price corridor first, then manage country-level reimbursement access.

1
Immediate

Confirm Germany-first launch sequence - do not file simultaneously in France

The scenario analysis shows a €630M five-year revenue difference between the Germany-first and France-first sequences under base case HTA assumptions. The current operational plan - simultaneous DE/FR submission - sits in the middle but sacrifices the German free-pricing period unnecessarily. Filing in Germany first, allowing the German list price to establish as an IRP reference in the twelve months before the AMNOG negotiation concludes, gives the company its best chance of a defensible European price corridor. The France submission timeline should be moved to follow German regulatory filing by a minimum of six months.

Expected impact: protect up to €630M in 5-year cumulative revenue versus the France-first base-case sequence.

2
Immediate

Stress-test the French dossier for ASMR III - accept that ASMR IV is a real scenario

The French medical team's current planning assumption is ASMR II (important improvement). Based on the precedent analysis of PFS-primary oncology submissions with immature OS data, ASMR II in France for this product profile is the optimistic scenario, not the base case. The market access strategy needs to be stress-tested against ASMR III as the base case and ASMR IV as the downside. If ASMR IV is realised, the IRP cascade consequences should inform whether to delay French submission until OS data matures, potentially two years, rather than accept a price that becomes the binding European floor.

Expected impact: avoid a downside price corridor that could reduce European net revenue by roughly €28k per patient per year.

3
Short Term

Design the Netherlands managed entry agreement as a data-generating mechanism, not a price concession

The ZIN assessment will require a conditional reimbursement agreement. The standard approach is to negotiate a volume-based rebate. The better approach - and the one with precedent in recent Dutch oncology managed entry agreements - is to structure the outcome-based agreement around real-world OS data collection, with the rebate triggered only if OS benefit is not confirmed at a defined interim analysis. This converts the Dutch conditional reimbursement from a price reduction into a data collection mechanism that also strengthens the OS evidence base for future AMNOG re-assessment and French re-negotiation.

Expected impact: preserve access in the Netherlands while generating real-world evidence that can improve later price re-negotiation positions.

4
Ongoing

Build a live IRP monitoring model - update quarterly as launch proceeds

The IRP cascade is not a static risk that is managed once at launch. As more markets reference the anchor prices and as rebate agreements are disclosed or inferred by other payers, the effective net price in reference markets moves. A quarterly IRP monitoring model - tracking the disclosed or estimated prices in Germany, France, and the Netherlands and applying them through the 26-market reference basket - should become a standing market access deliverable. This also informs the timing of market entry decisions in the reference markets themselves, where delay may be commercially rational if the reference price environment is deteriorating.

Expected impact: prevent unmanaged reference-price leakage and provide quarterly management visibility on price corridor movement.

Model Notes

Assumptions and limitations

The model uses analogue-based assumptions for HTA outcomes and net prices. Actual outcomes depend on dossier quality, comparator choice, payer negotiation dynamics, and final clinical data maturity.

Net prices are estimated because many European rebates are confidential. The IRP cascade therefore represents a directional commercial risk model rather than a precise price forecast.

Patient pool and uptake assumptions are based on analogue launch curves and therapy-area prevalence. Actual uptake may be constrained by diagnostic testing rates, guideline adoption, and local reimbursement criteria.

The Germany-first recommendation protects price corridor value, but it may create access delays in other markets. The final decision should balance commercial value, patient access, and organisational readiness.
