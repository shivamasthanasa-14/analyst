Why Is This Batch Failing? - Root Cause Analysis at a GMP Manufacturer

A GMP-certified German pharmaceutical manufacturer was rejecting 12% of production batches - triple the industry benchmark. The data existed across four systems. No one had connected it to a cause.

At an estimated €250k cost per failed commercial batch, the excess failure volume represents roughly €5M of avoidable annual quality cost before supply disruption or regulatory risk is considered.

Context

Why this problem matters

In pharmaceutical manufacturing, a failed batch is not simply a production loss. Every rejection triggers a deviation report, a quality investigation, and a review by the site's QA function. If the batch was destined for a regulated market, it may also require regulatory notification. The cumulative cost - direct material, labour, QA investigation time, and supply gap risk - typically runs to several hundred thousand euros per failed batch at commercial scale.

A 12% batch failure rate at a GMP-certified site is a signal that demands investigation. The industry reference point, drawn from EMA GMP compliance data and published manufacturing quality benchmarks, puts the expected range for solid oral dosage forms at 3–4%. Failures beyond that range typically indicate a systematic process problem rather than random variation.

The challenge at this site was not a lack of data. The manufacturing execution system (MES), the laboratory information management system (LIMS), the equipment maintenance logs, and the environmental monitoring system were all capturing data continuously. The problem was that nobody had structured a cross-system view to ask: which batches are failing, under what conditions, and what do those conditions have in common?

The Situation

What the site was facing

The site produces twelve product lines across three manufacturing suites - solid oral dosage forms (tablets and capsules) in Suites A and B, and a semi-solid topical line in Suite C. Over the eighteen months preceding the analysis, the quality team had recorded 47 batch failures across 392 production batches. That is a failure rate of 12.0% - roughly three times the expected rate for the product types being manufactured.

"Quality had been logging every deviation. Operations had been logging every equipment event. Nobody had asked what they looked like together."

Each failure had a deviation report. Each report had a stated cause. But the stated causes - "operator error," "equipment malfunction," "raw material variability" - had been recorded at the incident level, not analysed across incidents for pattern. The business problem was not only the 12% failure rate. It was the inability to separate random deviations from repeatable, preventable failure patterns.

Analysis · Step 1

What failure modes are actually occurring?

The first step was to reclassify the 47 deviation reports from their original stated causes into standardised failure mode categories. The original records used inconsistent terminology - what one operator logged as "blending issue" another had recorded as "content uniformity OOS." Mapping all records to four standardised categories was necessary before any frequency analysis was meaningful.

The four categories used follow the PDA Technical Report No. 54 classification: content uniformity (active ingredient distribution failures), dissolution/release profile (drug release outside specification), physical attributes (appearance, hardness, friability, weight variation), and microbiological/environmental (sterility or bioburden exceedances). The distribution of failures across these categories shapes where the investigation goes next.

The failure profile is not random Content uniformity failures account for 53% of all rejections. Dissolution failures add a further 26%. Physical attribute failures are close to the expected rate for the product type. Microbiological failures are minimal. The failure signature points clearly at the wet granulation process in Suite A and the equivalent blending stage in Suite B.
Analysis · Step 2

When and under what conditions are failures clustering?

With content uniformity and dissolution failures identified as the dominant modes, the next question was whether they were distributed randomly across time and production conditions, or whether they clustered. Clustering indicates assignable cause; random distribution suggests either measurement issues or inherent process capability problems.

Three variables were examined against the failure record: production shift (morning, afternoon, night), the age of the granulator equipment at the time of production, and the time elapsed since the granulator's last preventive maintenance service. The shift and equipment variables were cross-referenced using maintenance logs and the MES batch record timestamps.

A granulator in its oldest quartile running at night is the highest-risk configuration in the plant - and it is not being managed as such.
Analysis · Step 3

What is the role of maintenance intervals?

The shift and equipment age analysis identified the highest-risk configuration. The next question was whether preventive maintenance scheduling was a contributing factor - specifically, whether failure probability increased as the time since last PM service increased, and whether that effect was large enough to constitute an independent root cause.

PM records for the eight granulators across Suites A and B were extracted and matched to batch failure events by equipment ID and production date. The PM schedule called for servicing every 90 days. Actual intervals were calculated from the records. The analysis then bucketed failures by how many days post-PM the batch was produced.

Maintenance intervals are a confirmed risk factor Failure rate within 30 days of a PM service runs at approximately 5% - close to the industry benchmark. Between 30 and 60 days it rises to 9%. Beyond 90 days - which the PM schedule defines as the maximum interval - failures run at 18%. Eleven of the 18 month period's batches were produced on equipment more than 90 days past its scheduled PM. Eight of those eleven failed. The PM schedule is not being adhered to, and the consequence in failure rate terms is substantial. This is a separate root cause from the shift pattern, not an alternative explanation.
Analysis · Step 4

Building the root cause picture

Three independent variables have now been shown to correlate with elevated failure rates: failure mode concentration in granulation-related categories; night shift production on older equipment; and PM interval overruns. The final analytical step was to determine how these factors compound - because the question for management is not "which of these is the root cause" but "how much of the 12% failure rate can each factor account for, and in what combination?"

An FMEA-style risk scoring was applied to each identified factor using ICH Q9 methodology: severity (impact on patient safety and product quality), occurrence (frequency based on batch records), and detectability (whether the condition is currently flagged before a batch fails). Each factor was scored on a 1-10 scale, then multiplied to create the risk priority number (RPN). This makes the prioritisation logic explicit rather than relying on frequency alone.

Recommendations

What the analysis points to

Four actions follow from the root cause findings. They are ordered by speed of impact and controllability: actions that can immediately reduce failed batches are prioritised before longer-term investigations and capital planning.

1
Immediate

Enforce PM schedule adherence - halt batches on overdue equipment

PM interval overruns are the highest-RPN root cause and the only one fully within operations' control today. A policy should be implemented immediately: no batch starts on a granulator more than 90 days past its last PM. A real-time equipment status dashboard linked to the CMMS - showing days to next service for each unit - is the operational tool that makes this enforceable without additional labour. Expected failure rate reduction: 3–4 percentage points based on the overrun batch analysis.

2
Short Term

Investigate and close the night shift quality gap

A 2–2.5× higher failure rate on night shift, consistent across equipment age groups, points to a supervision, training, or process adherence gap - not a random effect. The investigation should compare the granulation process records between shifts for in-process check compliance, operator certification levels, and supervisor-to-operator ratios. The hypothesis that night shift operators are less rigorously following the critical process parameters for blending time and granulation endpoint should be tested directly against the MES process records.

Expected impact: 2-3 percentage point failure rate reduction if night-shift deviation rates are brought within 20% of morning-shift performance.

3
Medium Term

Develop a capital plan for granulator replacement in the oldest equipment quartile

The oldest equipment quartile is amplifying the effects of both the PM overrun and the night shift gap. Equipment age alone does not cause failures - but aged equipment is less tolerant of process variability and maintenance slippage. The site engineering team should produce a replacement cost-benefit analysis within 90 days, modelling the failure rate reduction achievable through granulator replacement against the capital cost, with NPV calculated at the current cost per failed batch.

Expected impact: €0.8M-€1.3M annual avoided failure cost, with additional upside from lower maintenance interventions and improved schedule reliability.

4
Ongoing

Launch a targeted supplier quality investigation into raw material particle size variation

The residual ~20% of unexplained excess failures does not cluster by equipment, shift, or maintenance interval. The two active ingredient suppliers showing the widest particle size distribution variation should be the starting point. Incoming material test data from the past 18 months should be matched against the failure event dates to test whether OOS batches correlate with specific supplier lots. This is a longer-duration investigation but addresses the only identified failure driver not yet confirmed or ruled out.

Expected impact: reduce residual unexplained failures by up to 20% if supplier lot variability is confirmed and controlled.

Model Notes

Assumptions and limitations

The batch-level dataset is illustrative and calibrated to published GMP manufacturing benchmarks. Actual site-level deviation data is proprietary and would require validated MES, LIMS, CMMS, and QA records for operational use.

Cost impact assumes €250k per failed commercial batch, including material, labour, QA investigation time, and lost production capacity. It excludes potential regulatory costs, backorder penalties, and customer supply risk.

FMEA scores are directional and intended for prioritisation. Final CAPA planning would require QA validation, process owner review, and documented investigation under the site pharmaceutical quality system.

The residual supplier variability hypothesis is not confirmed in this case. It is identified as the next testable workstream, not as a proven root cause.
