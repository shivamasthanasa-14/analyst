Heat or Heat Pump? Building a Decision Framework for Property Owners

Germany's heating law has turned heating replacement into a high-stakes decision for property owners. This case turns a confusing mix of subsidies, building suitability, district heating availability, and operating costs into a practical decision framework.

Key takeaway The right heating decision is not driven by technology preference alone. In the four profiles modelled here, net investment ranges from €8,400 to more than €38,000, while payback ranges from 6 years to a staged retrofit decision. A structured advisory framework prevents costly misrecommendations and reduces avoidable advisory time.

Context

Why this problem matters

Germany's Building Energy Act (Gebäudeenergiegesetz, GEG), revised in January 2024, requires new heating systems to operate on at least 65% renewable energy. Combined with the BEG subsidy programme administered through KfW, this has created a major decision point for property owners.

The challenge is not whether low-carbon options exist. Heat pumps, district heating connections, and hybrid systems can all be viable. The challenge is matching the right pathway to the right property. Building age, insulation standard, district heating availability, owner type, income eligibility, and current system age all change the answer.

For utility advisory teams, this is an operational problem as much as a customer problem. Without a shared framework, advisors spend too long reconstructing the logic, customers receive inconsistent recommendations, and subsidy timing can be misunderstood. This case builds the decision logic and cost model that should sit behind those advisory conversations.

The Situation

The problem the advisory team was facing

The utility's customer advisory team was receiving between 80 and 120 enquiries per week from property owners asking some version of the same question: "I need to replace my heating system, what should I do?" Calls were taking 45 to 60 minutes because advisors were reconstructing the logic from scratch each time. Different advisors were giving different recommendations for similar property profiles, and customers were often deciding too early, waiting too long, or misunderstanding subsidy eligibility.

The behavioural pattern was clear: customers usually contacted the utility only after a trigger event, such as boiler failure, a contractor quote, a media article on the heating law, or a subsidy deadline. By that point, the conversation was already emotional and time-sensitive. The advisory process needed to create structure quickly, not add more complexity.

"Every conversation starts from zero. We spend 20 minutes just establishing the basics before we can even start advising. There is no shared framework - each advisor has their own mental model."

Three failure modes kept appearing. Some customers were being advised to install heat pumps in properties that were genuinely unsuitable, especially poorly insulated pre-1980 buildings where operating efficiency would be weak. Others were told to wait for municipal heating plans even when district heating coverage was unlikely, risking missed subsidy timing. A third group, landlords of multi-unit buildings, was receiving advice designed for owner-occupiers, with the wrong subsidy logic applied.

The business problem was not simply customer confusion. It was the lack of a repeatable decision process that could separate technology suitability, subsidy eligibility, timing, and cost impact in the first ten minutes of the conversation.

Analysis · Step 1

What the decision actually depends on - and in what order

The first task was to establish the correct decision sequence. Property owners and advisors instinctively jump to cost comparisons - "which option is cheaper?" - before establishing which options are actually viable for a given property. Running cost scenarios for a heat pump that cannot work efficiently in a specific property produces misleading numbers and bad advice.

The framework establishes four sequential questions. Each one either opens or closes specific pathways before the next question is asked. This prevents the advisor from comparing options that are not genuinely comparable for the property in question.

The decision score is built from weighted factors: building suitability (35%), district heating availability and timing (25%), subsidy eligibility (25%), and operating cost advantage (15%). This keeps the recommendation grounded in viability first, then economics.

Why sequencing matters The most common advisory error was skipping Question 1 - building suitability - and going straight to subsidy eligibility. A poorly insulated 1970s building with radiators designed for 70°C flow temperatures cannot operate a heat pump efficiently at the 35–45°C flow temperatures modern heat pumps require. Running a cost comparison for such a property that shows heat pump "wins" on operating costs is only valid if the building is first retrofitted - which adds €15,000–€40,000 to the cost that is often absent from the comparison. The framework forces this question first.

Analysis · Step 3

What subsidy actually changes the numbers

The BEG subsidy structure is layered - a base rate plus up to three bonuses that stack depending on the property and owner profile. The maximum combined rate is 70%, capped at €30,000 eligible costs per housing unit, giving a maximum subsidy of €21,000 for a single-family home. But most property owners receive significantly less than the maximum because they do not qualify for all bonuses simultaneously.

The four profiles attract very different effective subsidy rates. Profile D - owner-occupier with income below €40,000 replacing an operational gas boiler before 2028 - qualifies for all four components: base 30% + efficiency bonus 5% + speed bonus 20% + income bonus 30% = 85%, but capped at 70%. Profile C - a landlord replacing a 14-year-old boiler - qualifies only for base 30% plus efficiency bonus 5% = 35%.

The subsidy changes the ranking - but not always in the way customers expect Profile D, despite having the newest gas boiler (only 3 years old), has the strongest financial case for a heat pump - not because the technology economics are compelling today, but because the subsidy stack is at its maximum and the 2028 speed bonus deadline creates a genuine time value. Profile B has the weakest case despite the oldest system, because the retrofit dependency means the total investment needed - heat pump plus building envelope - is €50,000 before subsidy, of which only the heat pump portion qualifies for BEG.
Analysis · Step 4

Annual operating costs and payback - where the decision closes

Capital cost comparisons alone do not determine the right choice. A higher upfront investment with significantly lower operating costs can have a shorter payback period than a cheaper installation with higher running costs. The operating cost comparison uses the following verified 2024 price references: electricity at €0.30/kWh, gas at €0.09/kWh, heating oil at €0.085/litre equivalent, and district heating at €0.11/kWh delivered heat. Heat pump efficiency is modelled at COP 3.5 for well-suited buildings and COP 2.2 for poorly suited ones - the difference this makes to the operating cost comparison is decisive.

Recommendations

What this means for the advisory team

Recommendations are prioritised by implementation speed, risk reduction, and repeatability. The immediate goal is to prevent wrong advice in live customer conversations. The second goal is to turn the framework into a scalable operating model.

1
Immediate

Deploy the four-question framework as a mandatory first step in every advisory conversation

The framework takes under ten minutes to apply and eliminates the three most common advisory failure modes. Expected impact: reduce average call time by 15 to 20 minutes, improve consistency across advisors, and avoid high-cost misrecommendations where retrofit dependency or district heating timing is missed.

2
Short Term

Build a digital tool that runs the cost model in real time during advisory calls

The cost-benefit model developed in this case is currently a static Excel file. Converting it into a web-based tool would let advisors populate customer inputs during the call, generate a documented recommendation, and reduce manual calculation effort. Expected impact: 25% to 35% lower advisory handling time once scaled across weekly enquiry volumes.

3
Medium Term

Differentiate advisory protocols for landlords vs owner-occupiers

Landlord cases (Profile C type) require a separate advisory track. The subsidy stack, billing implications, and DH versus heat pump economics differ from owner-occupier cases. Expected impact: fewer incorrect subsidy assumptions, clearer multi-unit recommendations, and lower escalation risk when tenants or landlords challenge cost allocation.

4
Ongoing

Update the cost model annually as energy prices, subsidy rates, and installation costs evolve

The BEG programme has been revised multiple times since 2021. Installation costs fell by around €4,000 in 2025, and electricity prices have declined from their 2022 peak. Expected impact: keeping the model current prevents outdated recommendations and protects advisor credibility when subsidy rates, energy prices, or installation costs change.

Assumptions & Limitations

What this model assumes

Model boundaries
Installation costs are based on published market ranges and representative property profiles, not contractor quotes for a specific building. Subsidy eligibility is modelled using BEG rules and simplified owner categories. Actual eligibility can change based on application timing, technical specifications, household income documentation, and programme updates.
Operating cost assumptions
Energy prices use 2024 references for electricity, gas, oil, and district heating. Payback periods do not model future price volatility, financing costs, maintenance differences, or carbon price changes. Heat pump efficiency assumes appropriate system design and installation quality.
Decision framework limitation
The framework is designed for first-stage advisory triage. It should guide the customer to the right pathway, but it does not replace a technical building assessment, installer quote, or formal subsidy approval.
