Where Is the Grid Leaking Money?

A German distribution network operator has watched grid losses and unplanned outages climb for four consecutive years. The issue is no longer only technical. Excess losses now represent an estimated €8.3M annual cost, with 71% of the gap concentrated in one zone.

This case shows how zone-level diagnostics, benchmark comparison, and cost modelling can turn a vague infrastructure concern into a clear investment priority.

Context

Why this problem matters

Germany's electricity grid is highly reliable at national level, but national averages can hide serious distribution-level problems. A DSO may look acceptable in aggregate while one regional zone is quietly driving most of the service quality and cost deterioration.

Grid losses are a direct financial cost because the operator must replace electricity that enters the network but does not reach customers. When outages rise at the same time, the problem also becomes a service, regulatory, and customer trust issue.

This case examines a representative German DSO with three regional zones: North, Central, and South. The business problem is not simply that losses are rising. It is that management does not know whether the cause is asset age, renewable integration, operational inefficiency, or a combination of all three.

Data Foundation

Data sources and their role in this analysis

Operational data for individual DSOs is proprietary. The benchmarks used here are drawn from verified public sources. Asset-level figures are synthetic but constructed to be consistent with published German DSO performance ranges and Bundesnetzagentur monitoring data.

The diagnostic logic combines three layers: loss rate deviation from benchmark, outage performance against SAIDI, and zone characteristics such as asset age, underground cabling share, and renewable penetration. This creates a practical prioritisation model rather than a simple trend review.

The Situation

Four years of rising losses, no clear explanation

The DSO's operations team had been tracking an uncomfortable trend. Grid losses - the gap between electricity fed into the network and electricity delivered to customers - had risen from 4.9% in 2020 to 6.8% in 2023, well above the German national average of 5.6%. Unplanned outage minutes per customer had climbed from 10.2 minutes to 17.4 minutes over the same period, now 36% above the national SAIDI benchmark of 12.8 minutes.

"Losses are up nearly two percentage points in four years. The regulator is asking questions. The board wants an explanation. Nobody has one that holds across all three zones."

The company operated three network zones - North, Central, and South - each with distinct characteristics. North was the largest zone by circuit length, with high rural coverage and the highest share of distributed renewable generation (wind and solar feeding back into the medium-voltage network). Central covered a mix of urban and industrial customers with the highest load density. South was the most recently upgraded zone, with a higher proportion of underground cabling.

The operations team's hypothesis was that aging infrastructure was the primary driver. But the data told a more complicated story. Without a structured analysis, it was impossible to know where investment would actually make a difference.

The core decision problem is therefore one of allocation: should capital be spread across the full network, targeted toward the oldest assets, or focused only on the zone where technical losses and outage minutes are compounding into measurable financial impact?

Analysis · Step 1

Four years of data, three zones - does the trend hold everywhere?

The first question was whether the deterioration in losses and outages was a portfolio-wide phenomenon or concentrated in specific zones. A company-wide average that masks zone-level divergence leads to unfocused investment - spending on infrastructure across the whole network when the problem is actually acute in one place.

The loss rate trend from 2020 to 2023 was disaggregated by zone. The result was not what the operations team expected.

Priority is assessed using a simple zone risk logic: loss rate above benchmark, deterioration over time, SAIDI deviation, and concentration of excess cost. This ensures the analysis points to where management action is most urgent, not just where the headline metric looks weak.

What the trend reveals The loss rate deterioration is not uniform. South Zone has remained stable and below the national benchmark throughout - its recent infrastructure upgrades and high underground cabling ratio are holding. North Zone is the primary problem, and its characteristics - oldest assets, highest renewable penetration, longest circuit length - point to a more specific root cause than simple aging.
Analysis · Step 2

Where are customers losing power - and for how long?

Grid losses represent a financial cost to the operator. Outages represent a service quality failure with regulatory and reputational consequences. The Bundesnetzagentur tracks SAIDI - System Average Interruption Duration Index - as the primary reliability metric for German DSOs. The national figure for 2023 was 12.8 minutes per customer. The company's overall figure was 17.4 minutes - above benchmark. But again, the zone-level picture is what matters.

SAIDI was disaggregated by zone and by fault category - equipment failure, third-party interference (dig-ins, vehicle strikes), weather-related, and planned maintenance. The separation of fault categories is important for the same reason as the downtime categorisation in portfolio analysis: the response to equipment failure is different from the response to dig-in incidents, and investment in cable protection schemes is different from investment in asset replacement.

Analysis · Step 3

What the losses actually cost

Network losses have a direct financial cost - the DSO must procure replacement energy to cover electricity that entered the network but was dissipated as heat or lost through technical inefficiencies. In Germany, this is called Verlustenergie (loss energy). The Bundesnetzagentur's monitoring report confirmed that TSO-level loss energy procurement cost €1.3 billion in 2023 across the national network - up from €0.8 billion in 2022 despite lower volumes, due to elevated reference prices.

At the DSO level, the cost calculation uses the volume of losses in MWh multiplied by the average procurement price for loss energy. For a mid-sized DSO feeding approximately 4.2 TWh annually into its distribution network, the difference between a 5.6% national average loss rate and the current 6.8% is not trivial.

The financial case for North Zone prioritisation North Zone's excess losses cost €5.9M annually in loss energy procurement alone. This figure does not include the cost of unplanned outages - crew dispatch, customer compensation under regulatory frameworks, and reputational impact. It also does not include the regulatory risk of continued SAIDI deterioration above benchmark, which in Germany triggers increased Bundesnetzagentur scrutiny and can affect the DSO's permitted revenue under incentive regulation. The financial case for prioritising North Zone investment is clear before a single infrastructure cost is modelled.
Analysis · Step 4

Infrastructure age alone does not explain it - renewable integration does

The operations team's original hypothesis was straightforward: assets are aging, losses are rising, replace the assets. But if asset age were the primary driver, Central Zone - with a 26-year average asset age - would show a more similar loss profile to North Zone's 32-year assets. It does not. And South Zone's improvement after infrastructure upgrades supports the investment case but does not explain why North Zone's trajectory is steeper than Central's age profile would predict.

The additional variable is renewable penetration. North Zone has 28% distributed renewable generation, predominantly wind and solar, feeding back into the medium-voltage network. This bidirectional power flow creates voltage volatility that increases technical losses and stresses equipment that was designed for unidirectional load delivery. The combination of aging assets and high renewable penetration creates a compounding effect that neither factor alone would produce.

The operational pattern also matters. Equipment failure creates outage minutes, while voltage volatility increases technical losses before a visible failure occurs. That means the highest-cost risk may not always appear first as a breakdown. It can appear as a steady loss-rate increase that only becomes obvious once the data is viewed by zone.

The real diagnosis - not aging infrastructure, but aging infrastructure under renewable stress North Zone's loss problem is not simply that its assets are old. Central Zone's assets are also aging, but its lower renewable penetration means they operate under more predictable, unidirectional load conditions. North Zone's 32-year-old overhead lines and transformers were not designed for the bidirectional voltage swings introduced by 28% distributed renewable generation. The investment priority is therefore not a blanket asset replacement programme across the whole network - it is targeted replacement of the specific asset classes in North Zone most vulnerable to voltage volatility: medium-voltage transformers and overhead line sections in high-renewable-penetration feeder areas. This is a different investment brief - more specific, more defensible, and more cost-effective - than the operations team's original hypothesis would have produced.
Recommendations

Where investment should go

Four actions follow from the analysis. They are ordered by financial impact, urgency, and controllability. The first priority is to address the zone where losses are both concentrated and financially material, while using lower-cost operational measures to stabilise performance during the investment cycle.

1
Immediate

Targeted asset replacement in North Zone high-renewable feeder areas

The €5.9M annual excess loss cost in North Zone provides a clear investment threshold. Medium-voltage transformers and overhead line sections on feeders with above-average renewable backfeed should be prioritised for replacement or reinforcement. These assets are operating under conditions they were not designed for, and their failure mode is accelerating. The investment case closes in under two years at current loss energy prices.

Expected impact: reduce excess loss cost by €3M to €4M annually once priority feeders are reinforced, depending on procurement prices and asset replacement sequencing.

2
Short Term

Voltage management programme for North Zone medium-voltage network

Asset replacement takes time and capital. A faster interim measure is active voltage management through on-load tap changers and reactive power compensation on the most stressed feeders in North Zone. This does not eliminate the loss problem but dampens the voltage volatility that is accelerating equipment stress and increasing technical losses. Estimated cost: €0.8M to €1.2M. Estimated loss reduction: 0.4 to 0.6 percentage points while the capital programme is executed.

Expected impact: €0.7M to €1.1M annual loss cost reduction as an interim measure before full asset replacement is complete.

3
Medium Term

Third-party interference reduction in North Zone overhead network

North Zone's SAIDI contribution from third-party interference (7.8 minutes) is disproportionately high relative to Central and South. The overhead line network in rural areas is exposed to vegetation incursion and vehicle strikes. A targeted overhead-to-underground conversion programme on the highest-incident feeder sections, combined with a vegetation management schedule, would reduce this component of outage minutes. The Bundesnetzagentur's network expansion data confirms that underground conversion is a standard DSO resilience investment in Germany's current regulatory environment.

Expected impact: reduce SAIDI by 3 to 5 minutes in North Zone and lower emergency crew dispatch costs by focusing on the highest-incident feeder sections.

4
Ongoing

Establish zone-level KPI monitoring with quarterly reporting to the board

The four-year trend in losses and outages was visible in the data throughout, but was only being reviewed at the company aggregate level. Zone-level SAIDI, loss rate, and loss energy cost should be tracked monthly, with quarterly board reporting. The South Zone improvement story shows that investment works and is measurable. The same discipline applied to North Zone will make the impact of intervention visible and provide the evidence base for the next regulatory review period.

Expected impact: earlier detection of zone-level deterioration and stronger evidence for capex approval, regulatory discussions, and performance steering.

Model Notes

Assumptions and limitations

The analysis uses a representative DSO dataset calibrated to public German grid benchmarks. Actual DSO asset-level performance data is proprietary and not publicly disclosed.

Loss cost is calculated using an average loss energy procurement price of €85/MWh. Actual procurement costs may vary by contract structure, regulatory treatment, and market price movements.

SAIDI comparisons use national benchmarks from Bundesnetzagentur reporting. Regional geography, weather exposure, and network topology can create legitimate deviations from national averages.

The root cause diagnosis is based on observed relationships between asset age, renewable penetration, underground cabling share, loss rate, and outage performance. A final investment decision would require feeder-level engineering validation and asset condition testing.
